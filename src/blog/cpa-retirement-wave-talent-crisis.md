The accounting profession has weathered talent shortages before — hiring freezes, economic disruptions, generational gaps in workforce entry. But what's coming in the next decade is different in character and scale from anything the profession has previously experienced.

**75% of CPAs plan to retire within the next 15 years** (CPA Practice Advisor, 2025). Three-quarters of the profession's experienced practitioners — the ones who hold client relationships, institutional knowledge, and technical expertise built over careers — are planning to exit.

This isn't a talent shortage. It's a structural reckoning.

## 15 Years vs 3 Years: The Timeline Is Already Tight

The 15-year horizon makes the retirement wave sound manageable. The 3-year data tells a different story.

**One in five accounting firms expects to lose 50% or more of their staff to retirement within just three years** (CPA Practice Advisor, 2025). Not the 15-year window. Three years.

For these firms, succession planning isn't a future exercise — it's an immediate operational challenge. Partners who planned to transition in 5–7 years are now facing succession conversations they weren't prepared to have. The client relationships, institutional knowledge, and delivery capacity that retiring partners represent don't transfer automatically. They require deliberate, early planning to preserve.

The firms that started succession planning five years ago are prepared. The ones that didn't are now playing catch-up in a compressed timeline with a talent market that can't absorb the demand.

## Mega-Mergers: The Market's First Response

When individual firms can't solve succession internally, the market provides an external solution.

Baker Tilly and Moss Adams merged in April 2025, creating a **$3.5 billion firm**. This wasn't primarily a growth-through-scale merger. It was succession combined with scale — two strong firms building a combined entity capable of retaining partner-level talent, serving larger clients, and providing career trajectories that prevent the talent exodus both firms were experiencing individually.

The merger wave is accelerating. When firms can't replace retiring partners through hiring or internal promotion, they merge — combining client bases, talent pools, and operational infrastructure to solve succession problems that neither firm could solve independently.

For mid-market and smaller firms watching this consolidation, the strategic question is: how do we build the resilience to remain independent?

## The Pipeline Has Collapsed Simultaneously

The retirement wave would be manageable if the entry-level pipeline were flowing. It isn't.

**CPA candidates dropped 34% in a single year** — from 42,626 new candidates in 2023 to 28,082 in 2024 (AICPA 2025 Trends Report). The pipeline feeding the profession with new practitioners collapsed at precisely the moment retirements began accelerating.

The math is stark: the profession is losing experienced practitioners far faster than it's adding new ones. The total accounting workforce has already contracted 17% since 2020. The gap between departures and new entrants isn't closing — it's widening.

Individual firms cannot hire their way out of a profession-wide pipeline collapse. The talent simply isn't available in sufficient quantity, regardless of what any individual firm offers.

## Building Systems, Not Headcount Dependency

The firms that will survive the retirement wave and maintain competitive service delivery share one strategic characteristic: **they have built delivery systems that don't depend entirely on specific individuals**.

The firms most vulnerable to the retirement wave are those where client relationships, institutional knowledge, and service delivery are concentrated in senior partners. When those partners retire, they take the capability with them. The solution isn't finding equivalent people to replace them — it's building systems that preserve and deliver capability independent of specific individuals.

This is the core insight behind the rise of outsourced accounting partnerships, standardized workflow systems, documented processes, and AI-assisted delivery models. When quality and capability are embedded in systems rather than concentrated in people, retirements don't become crises.

**The outsourced accounting model contributes directly to this resilience**: by handling the volume processing, bookkeeping, reconciliation, and compliance work through a structured external team, firms reduce their dependence on in-house headcount for core delivery. The team doesn't retire. The system doesn't exit.

## The Strategic Response for CPA Firms

Building retirement-proof capacity requires several parallel efforts:

**Document and systematize institutional knowledge** before it walks out the door. Client histories, technical approaches, relationship context — captured in systems rather than stored only in people's heads.

**Build outsourced delivery capacity** for the high-volume compliance and processing work that drives workload but doesn't require senior expertise. This frees senior staff for the advisory and client relationship work that actually requires their expertise.

**Develop the next generation of advisory capability** deliberately, not by accident. The juniors who will eventually replace retiring partners need advisory experience — and they only get it if senior partners aren't doing all the advisory work themselves.

**Explore merger or aggregation options** proactively, while the firm has negotiating strength, rather than reactively when succession pressure has created urgency.
